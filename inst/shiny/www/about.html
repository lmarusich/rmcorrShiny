About this app

This Shiny app computes and visualizes repeated measures correlation (rmcorr). Rmcorr is the common within-individual linear association for paired, repeated measures data. Rmcorr is conceptually similar to a null multilevel model: A fixed slope and varying intercept by participant. For details about rmcorr and analyzing repeated measures data see below.

Example Data: Publication Sources and Description

  • bland1995:  Health science data with repeated measures, by participant, of pH (degree of base or acidity) and PaCO2 (partial pressure of carbon dioxide)
  • gilden2010:  Psychology data of reaction time and accuracy for a visual search task by participant, by repeated block (both dependent measures are averaged by block)
  • marusich2016:  Psychology data of dyads working together to capture High Value Targets (lower task time is better performance) and their averaged Mission Awareness Rating Scale (MARS) for each block, repeated three times. MARS evaluates subjective situation awareness ("knowing what is going on"), higher values indicate better situation awareness
  • raz2005:  Neuroscience data containing the volume of a brain area (cerebellar hemisphere) measured twice per participant at two different ages, approximately 5 years apart

Alternative to rmcorr: Resources for multilevel modeling

Mulitlevel modeling is a more complicated alternative to rmcorr, but multilevel modeling has capabilities that rmcorr does not. Multilevel modeling permits varying slopes whereas rmcorr always fits a common or fixed slope across participants. We recommend using multilevel modeling if: there are more than two variables of interest (in addition to participants or subjects), data with more than two levels, simultaneous modeling of within and between-participants is needed, visual patterns patterns suggest varying slopes by individual and/or non-linear associations, and time need to be explicitly included in the model (i.e., modeling change over time as a separate variable. See the Journal Article for rmcorr and references below for more information.

  • Website (Freeman). Intuitive visual explanation of multilevel modeling
  • Journal Article (Aarts et al., 2014). Excellent overview on why repeated measures should be modeled and overview of multilevel modeling
  • Conference paper (Singmann, 2018). Informative tutorial on multilevel modeling
  • Book (Gelman and Hill, 2006). Comprehensive book on multilevel modeling with detailed explanations, equations, and examples using real data
